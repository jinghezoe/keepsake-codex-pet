# Privacy, consent, and memorial care

Turning a person into a pet is technically an illustration task, but socially it is a likeness and memory project. Treat it accordingly.

## Living people

- Ask clearly before using their photos.
- Explain where the result will live and whether it will be public.
- Let them reject frames or details that feel embarrassing, inaccurate, or too private.
- Do not publish raw photos merely because the final pet was approved.
- Do not infer sensitive traits from appearance.

## Memorial projects

- Keep the first version private by default.
- Consult close family or relevant rights holders before publishing a recognizable likeness.
- Avoid making the pet speak new opinions, deliver messages, imitate a voice, or simulate consent.
- Prefer small, verifiable memories: a garment, expression, gesture, or object.
- Allow grief to remain complicated; the pet should not claim to replace the person or resolve loss.

## Children and bystanders

Do not use a child's likeness without guardian permission. Crop or omit unrelated people in source images, and never publish photos containing private addresses, schools, badges, or identifying paperwork.

## Repository hygiene

Before a public push:

1. Search for raw photos and video.
2. Search for EXIF-bearing files.
3. Search for names, emails, local filesystem paths, tokens, and chat logs.
4. Confirm that every visible example image is approved for that audience.
5. State which files are covered by the repository license and which are not.

When uncertain, publish the workflow and an abstract demo, and keep the personal result local.
